Picture this: the year is 2045, and humanity, having grown weary of the tedious habit of aging, officially decides to become immortal. At least, that is the forecast from Raymond Kurzweil—a man who collects patents and honorary degrees with the same quiet fervor others might reserve for vintage stamps. His vision of the technological singularity suggests we will finally deign to merge with the machine, achieving a state of existence where the only real threat is a forced system update rather than the finality of death.

Kurzweil, whose pioneering work in speech and text recognition ensures our smartphones understand us better than our own families do, is convinced the day the calculator outsmarts its owner is nearly here. At that momentous threshold, our fragile bodies and the very fabric of civilization will transform so thoroughly that we’ll scarcely recognize ourselves in the mirror. And given the current pace of progress, this shift is likely to arrive well before most of us have cleared our mortgages.

Believers are already preparing for a future as "super-cyborgs," where a silicon chip is merely a natural extension of one’s thoughts and AI graciously handles the nuisance of biological decay. This entire grand affair is known as the Singularity—a hypothetical explosion of progress fueled by self-replicating machines and biotechnologies. It has evolved beyond mere science into a full-blown subculture, complete with a university sponsored by NASA and Google, and conferences in San Francisco that blend the gravity of a world economic forum with the wide-eyed enthusiasm of a UFO convention. Even Elon Musk, never one to miss a futuristic bandwagon, has joined the fray with his Optimus robot.

The idea itself is hardly a modern novelty. Back in 1965, the mathematician Irving Good described an "intelligence explosion," logically concluding that if a machine surpasses us, it will naturally be much better at designing the next generation of machines than we ever were. The result is a cascade of self-improvement that leaves the human mind trailing far behind in the intellectual dust. Our crowning achievement, it seems, will be to invent the very thing that renders our own invention unnecessary. The supporting arguments are straightforward: computing power is skyrocketing, there is no ceiling in sight, and eventually, the finish line—where a computer thinks deeper thoughts than the philosopher—will be crossed.
