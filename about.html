Personal experience is a stubborn thing: a regular bombardment of the brain with first-person flight dynamics once allowed me to lift off the ground in a state between sleep and wakefulness, moving on pure intent alone, without any external engines. Reality remained the same, yet the "jetpack" became a part of my internal setting. Twenty years later, the practice of "objective processes" confirmed this: the regular execution of simple commands—"look, touch, take"—indoctrinates consciousness far more deeply than any theory. We do not need artificial limbs to feel whole; we only need to habituate our consciousness to the steady illusion that they exist.
